Scheduling meetings across time zones without the headache

March 27, 2026

Cross-time-zone scheduling fails for two common reasons: someone guesses an offset by hand, or “9am” is sent without saying whose 9am. A few habits fix most problems.

Find the overlap

List each participant’s city, then use TimeZoneMeet to compare current local times. For a proposed slot, mentally (or on paper) place that wall-clock time in each city: would anyone be outside working hours? Adjust until the window is tolerable for everyone who must attend live.

Write times with a time zone

Instead of “9:00 Tuesday,” prefer “Tuesday 9:00 PT / 12:00 ET” or use a single reference such as “17:00 UTC.” If you use a city name, tie it to the invite: “9:00 in Los Angeles (see calendar for your local equivalent).”

Watch for DST changes

Not every region shifts clocks on the same dates. A recurring meeting that worked in winter can land in a bad slot after a daylight saving transition. Re-check near those dates, especially for global groups.

Quick checks before you send

  • Confirm each city’s current offset with a lookup tool rather than memorizing “X hours from me.”
  • If you use a “reasonable hours” band (for example roughly 5am–8pm local), check both the start and the end of the meeting in each place. A slot that starts at 7:30pm but runs 60 minutes pushes one side past 8:00pm.
  • If someone joins from a new location, re-run the comparison.
  • For more frequent lookups, TimeZoneMeet premium reduces friction with a higher suggestion limit and no ads.
